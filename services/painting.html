Why Paint Goes Wrong

Painting is 80% prep, 20% rolling. Most DIY jobs reverse that ratio.

The visible layer of paint is the easy part — it's the prep underneath that makes a paint job look professional or amateur. We've seen rooms repainted three times in a year because the underlying surface was wrong. We do the boring half so the visible half lasts.

The DIY pitfalls we see every week

  • Painting over a glossy surface without de-glossing. Latex paint won't bond to satin or semi-gloss without a deglosser, a light sand, or a bonding primer. The paint rolls on fine, dries fine, then peels in sheets six months later when the dog leans against the wall. Prep adds 30 minutes; skipping it costs you the whole repaint.
  • One coat of paint over a dark color, no primer. Going from navy to white needs at least primer + two coats, sometimes three. Skipping primer is why "this paint says one-coat coverage" never delivers — the can lies. We use a tinted primer to neutralize the existing color first.
  • Tape pulled off after the paint fully dries. Paint forms a film with the tape. If you wait until paint is dry to pull tape, you tear the paint film off with it. Pull tape while paint is still tacky (or score the edge with a razor first). Almost every "the line came off jagged" call is a tape-timing problem.
  • Roller marks visible at low light. Rolling paint requires the right nap thickness for the surface (3/8" for smooth walls, 1/2" for textured), a wet edge maintained the whole wall, and finishing strokes pulled in one direction top to bottom. Cheap rollers shed lint into the finish — you only see it under raking light from a window. We use Purdy or Wooster covers exclusively.
  • Bathroom paint that mildews in three months. Standard interior paint has zero mildew resistance. Humid Miami bathrooms, Atlanta basements, and Houston-style climates need kitchen-and-bath paint with a mildewcide additive. Otherwise you'll be repainting the ceiling around the shower every winter.

Tools and skills required

A pro paint job uses: 4-foot sash brush for cut-in, angled trim brush, premium rollers (Purdy White Dove or Wooster Pro Doo-Z), an extension pole, drop cloths (canvas, not plastic — paint pools on plastic), painter's tape (FrogTape Multi-Surface for crisp lines on textured walls), a 5-in-1 tool, sandpaper (220-grit for finish prep, 120-grit for fillers), painter's caulk for trim gaps, primer-tinted-to-color, two coats of finish paint. The skill part is in the prep diagnostics — knowing whether a wall needs sanding, deglossing, priming, or just a wipe-down before paint goes on.

Safety considerations and code

Two real safety items with paint: lead and ventilation. Any home built before 1978 may have lead-based paint somewhere in the layers, and the EPA RRP rule (Renovation, Repair and Painting) requires certified handling for lead disturbance. We test before sanding any pre-1978 home and we don't sand untested surfaces — we use chemical strippers and HEPA-filtered tools. The other piece is ventilation: even modern low-VOC paint needs cross-ventilation during application. We always open windows and run a small air mover for indoor jobs. For nurseries and asthma-sensitive households we use zero-VOC product (Benjamin Moore Aura or Sherwin-Williams Harmony) and do the work when the room can be unoccupied for 24 hours.

FAQ

Painting Questions

Can you match my existing paint color?

If you have the original paint can or a swatch, yes — we hand-match at the local Sherwin-Williams or Benjamin Moore. If you don't have either, we can take a 1-inch chip to the store for a spectro-match — it gets within 95% accuracy. For touch-ups on aged paint, we usually recommend repainting the entire wall corner-to-corner, because UV-faded paint never blends invisibly with new paint regardless of how good the match is.

Do you cover furniture and floors?

Yes — drop cloths on every floor surface, plastic on furniture we can't move, painter's tape on every edge. Move-out clean: outlet covers off and tagged, switch plates off, doorknobs masked. We bring everything; you don't have to prep.

How long does a single room take?

A standard 12x14 bedroom: prep + cut-in + two coats + cleanup is roughly 4–6 hours for one painter. Add an hour for ceiling, an hour for trim. We schedule a single room for a half-day, two rooms for a full day, and let the paint cure overnight before you move furniture back.

What kind of paint do you use?

For interior walls we default to Benjamin Moore Regal Select or Sherwin-Williams Cashmere — both are mid-tier pro-grade with excellent washability. For trim, semi-gloss latex (BM Advance or SW Emerald Urethane). For bathroom ceilings in humid markets like Miami or Atlanta, mildew-resistant kitchen-and-bath paint. We don't use builder-grade paint — it costs the same to apply and lasts a third as long.

Will you do exterior painting?

Touch-ups, doors, trim, and small accent areas yes. Full exterior repaints we typically subcontract to a licensed paint contractor because the scope (lift work, pressure washing, lead-paint testing on pre-1978 homes) is past handyman standards. We coordinate the project either way.

Can you remove wallpaper before painting?

Yes. Modern strippable wallpaper comes off cleanly. Older glue-down wallpaper takes a steamer, scoring tool, and patience — and the wall underneath usually needs a skim coat of joint compound before it can be painted. We quote that as a separate prep line because it's often more time than the painting itself.
