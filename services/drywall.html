Honest Talk

Drywall is easy to start. Hard to finish. That's why it sits unfinished.

Anybody can apply joint compound. Almost nobody gets it to disappear under a paint roller. The work is 90% sanding, blending, feathering, and lighting checks — and that's where every weekend warrior runs out of patience. We skip the patience problem because we do this every day.

The DIY pitfalls we see every week

  • Self-adhesive patch kits stuffed into 6-inch holes. The mesh patch is rated for up to roughly 4 inches in unsupported drywall. Past that, the patch flexes when you press on it, which cracks the joint compound the moment a door slams or a kid leans on the wall. The fix is a backed patch — a piece of drywall scrap glued behind the hole — and most homeowners don't know to do that.
  • Sanding done dry, indoors, with no plastic. Joint compound dust is fine, white, and gets into every electronic in your home. We tape off the room, vacuum-sand with a HEPA shroud, and we leave less mess than the before photo.
  • One coat of mud, painted over the same day. Joint compound shrinks as it dries — sometimes by 30%. A single thick pass cracks. The right way is two or three thin coats, sanded between, then primed before any color paint touches it. Skipping primer is why your patch always shows through topcoat as a "halo."
  • Doors planed instead of shimmed. Almost every door that drags or won't latch is fixable with a 3-inch screw replacing the top-hinge screw — pulls the jamb back into alignment in 90 seconds. Cutting the door is the last resort, not the first.
  • Caulk used where filler belongs. Caulk is for joints that move (wall-to-trim seam). It will not hold paint smoothly across a hole or a gouge. We use spackle or wood filler depending on substrate and we use the right product for each spot.

Tools and skills required

A drywall fix done correctly needs: 6-inch and 12-inch knives, a setting-type joint compound, regular pre-mix for finish coats, a corner trowel, a damp-sponge sanding block (replaces dust with controlled mess), a halogen work light to hit the patch at a low angle (the only way to see ridges), texture spray, primer, and the matching paint. The skill part is reading the wall — knowing if a hole is structural (behind a stud cavity) or skin-deep, and knowing when to sister a backing strip versus when a mesh patch is enough.

Safety and code considerations

Most drywall work is low-stakes — but not all. Anything cut into a wall in your kitchen or bathroom needs to clear plumbing and electrical before the saw goes in. We probe with a thin trim nail and use a non-contact AC voltage detector before any cut larger than a knife slot. In bathrooms and kitchens, we replace cut drywall with mold-resistant green-board (or cement board behind tile) — same material the original installer should have used, often didn't. Older homes (pre-1980) may also have plaster-and-lath instead of drywall, which we handle differently to avoid blowing out the surrounding plaster.

FAQ

Drywall & Repair Questions

How big a hole can you patch?

Anything from a doorknob ding to a full sheet replacement. Small holes (under 4 inches) get a self-adhesive mesh patch with two coats of joint compound. Medium holes (4–12 inches) get a California patch — a clean cut, a backing strip, and a paper-tape mud job. Anything larger gets a stud-anchored sister piece of drywall.

Can you match the texture of my walls?

Yes. We carry orange-peel and knockdown texture in spray cans for small spots. For larger patches we mix joint compound to match your wall's existing splatter or skip-trowel pattern. Smooth Level-5 finish (like new construction in Miami high-rises) takes longer because every imperfection shows — we plan for two thin coats and a sanding pass.

My door won't latch — can you fix it?

Almost always yes, and almost always in under 30 minutes. The fix is usually a strike-plate adjustment, a longer hinge screw to pull the jamb back, or a shim behind the top hinge. Doors that drag on the floor often just need the hinges tightened or a 1/8-inch trim. We carry a planer in the truck for the rare case it does.

Do you do crown molding and baseboards?

Baseboards, shoe molding, door casing, and chair rail — yes. Full crown molding installation across a room is also fine but it's a job we'll typically schedule as a dedicated project rather than a 30-minute fix because miter cuts on inside corners take time to do correctly.

Will you paint the patch when you're done?

Yes. We prime the patch and feather a paint blend if you have the paint code or a sample. If we're doing the patch as part of a member visit and you don't have the original paint, we recommend repainting wall-to-wall on the affected wall — touch-ups never disappear completely under raking light.

How long until the patch is fully dry and paintable?

Setting-type joint compound (the bag with the powder) cures by chemistry in 30–90 minutes regardless of humidity — we use it for fast jobs. Pre-mixed compound dries by evaporation and takes 24 hours per coat in normal conditions, longer in humid climates like Miami or Atlanta. We choose based on whether the patch needs same-day turnaround.
