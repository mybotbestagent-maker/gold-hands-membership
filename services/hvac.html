The Math

Skipped filter changes are why AC compressors die at year 7 instead of year 15.

HVAC is the most expensive system in your home and the easiest one to neglect. A new central AC unit runs $7,000–$14,000 installed. Properly maintained, that unit lasts 12–18 years. Neglected, the same unit dies in 6–9. The single biggest predictor of HVAC lifespan is whether somebody changes the filter on a schedule. We are that somebody.

The DIY pitfalls we see every week

  • "I changed it last month" filters that have been there 4 months. Filter neglect is the most common HVAC mistake we encounter, and homeowners genuinely don't realize how much time has passed. By the time the airflow noticeably drops, the coil already has months of dust accumulated on it. We log every filter change in member records so there's no guessing.
  • Wrong-MERV filters that strangle the system. A higher MERV rating sounds better, but a MERV-13 filter in a system designed for MERV-8 chokes the airflow and forces the blower motor to work harder. We size to your unit's spec, not to the marketing on the package. The right MERV for most residential systems is 8–11; MERV-13 is for HEPA-grade applications with the right blower.
  • Outdoor condenser units packed with leaves and grass clippings. The fins on the outdoor unit need clear airflow on all sides — typically 24 inches of clearance. We've pulled grocery-bags' worth of debris out of condensers and seen efficiency jump 30% from a 5-minute cleanup. In Miami, we also clean salt-air corrosion off the fins with a fin-friendly cleaner.
  • Condensate lines clogged with algae, water dripping inside. The PVC line that drains your AC's condensate clogs over time with biofilm and algae — especially in humid markets. The tell-tale signs: water spots on the ceiling below an attic air handler, or a "safety pan" full of standing water. We flush condensate lines with a vinegar treatment monthly.
  • Doors weatherstripping shot, AC running 30% harder than it needs to. A standard exterior door has a weatherstrip seal around three sides. After 6–8 years it compresses and stops sealing. The result is a small constant air leak that adds 10–30% to your monthly bill. Replacement is a 15-minute job per door. We catch these on seasonal walk-throughs.

Tools and skills required

Real seasonal maintenance needs: the right size filter (and tracking what size that is — there are 30+ common sizes), a wet/dry vacuum for condensate-line flushing, fin-comb for bent condenser fins, a coil-safe cleaner, a digital thermometer for measuring delta-T across the coil (15–20°F is healthy; anything outside that range is a refrigerant or airflow issue), weatherstripping in V-strip and rolled-vinyl varieties, calk and caulk gun, and a logbook of what was done when. The skill part is reading the system's symptoms — knowing whether short-cycling is a thermostat issue, an oversized unit, or low refrigerant — and knowing when to escalate to a licensed HVAC tech for refrigerant work.

Code, licensing, and refrigerant work

We are explicit about scope here. Anything that involves opening the refrigerant loop — recharging, leak repair, compressor replacement, coil replacement — is licensed HVAC work in every state we serve. The EPA Section 608 certification is required by federal law to handle refrigerant. We don't do that work; we coordinate with a licensed HVAC partner for it. What we do is the entire scope around it: filters, condensate, drainage, airflow, thermostats, blower motor checks, weatherstripping, and seasonal walk-throughs. Done right, this work prevents 90% of the calls that send people to a licensed tech in the first place.

FAQ

HVAC & Seasonal Questions

How often should I really change my AC filter?

Every 30–60 days for most homes; every 30 days for homes with pets, smokers, or in high-pollen markets. In Miami's humid summer your filter loads up faster than the ASHRAE textbook says — we've pulled filters that were essentially solid grey felt after 6 weeks. A clogged filter forces your AC to work harder, which is the #1 cause of premature compressor failure.

What's a condensate line and why does it matter?

Your AC's evaporator coil pulls humidity out of the air. That water has to drain somewhere — through a small PVC line called the condensate line. In hot/humid climates these lines clog with algae and biofilm. A clogged condensate line trips a float switch (best case) or floods your ceiling (worst case). We flush these with a vinegar-based treatment monthly for member homes.

Can you replace a thermostat?

Yes — including smart thermostats like Nest, Ecobee, and Honeywell T-series. The two issues we always check first: (1) does your existing wiring include a C wire? Most smart thermostats need one for power. (2) Is your system high-voltage millivolt or low-voltage 24V? About 95% of modern systems are 24V and we install no problem; older boiler-only zones sometimes need an adapter.

Is a tune-up the same as a check-up?

No. Our member visits include filter swap, condensate flush, drain pan inspection, return-vent check, and a thermostat-cycle test. A full HVAC tune-up — where a tech opens the unit, measures refrigerant pressures, cleans coils, and checks compressor amperage — is licensed-HVAC scope and we coordinate that with a partner annually. We tell you when your system is due for one.

What's included in 'seasonal maintenance' beyond AC?

Spring: AC pre-season check, gutter clean, exterior caulk inspection. Summer: filter swap, condensate flush, ceiling-fan reverse for cooling. Fall: heat pre-season check, weatherstripping refresh, dryer vent clean (huge fire-prevention win). Winter: pipe insulation in cold-climate cities, gutter heat-cable test, bath fan check. We adjust the seasonal calendar to your specific climate and home — Miami's checklist is very different from Chicago's.

Will you do hurricane prep?

Yes — for our Miami, Tampa, and Atlantic-coast members. Pre-season we check hurricane shutters, garage door bracing, roof tie-downs, and outdoor anchor points; we secure or remove anything that could become a projectile. Post-storm we do priority damage assessment so insurance documentation is filed quickly. This is one of the highest-value pieces of the membership for hurricane-zone homeowners.
