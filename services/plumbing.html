Real Talk

Why "small" plumbing leaks become $8,000 mold remediation jobs.

The single most expensive lesson in homeownership is that a $1.50 rubber gasket, when ignored, becomes a $9,000 floor replacement. Plumbing has the worst leverage of any home system — small problem ignored, big problem inevitable. We catch the small ones on a schedule.

If you have an active leak right now: Shut the water off at the angle stop under the fixture (or at the main if you can't find the angle stop), put towels down, and call us. Members on Silver and Gold get priority dispatch; Bronze members and non-members can call for next-available.

The DIY pitfalls we see every week

  • Overtightened compression fittings, cracked porcelain. The biggest single mistake on toilet repairs is wrenching down on the supply line and cracking the toilet tank. Hand-tight plus a quarter-turn is the rule. We've replaced more than a few toilets that started as a $25 fill-valve job.
  • 10-year-old supply lines that failed during the night. Braided stainless supply lines have a finite life — typically 8–10 years. They fail explosively, full open, and a single failed line can dump 6 gallons per minute until somebody finds the shutoff. We replace them on a schedule for members and we tag the install date on the new ones.
  • P-trap leaks reglued instead of replaced. If a P-trap is leaking at the slip nut, the washer is shot. Adding pipe dope or teflon tape doesn't fix it — replacing the trap kit ($6 at Home Depot, 10 minutes of labor) does.
  • Faucet aerators full of mineral scale, blamed on "low water pressure." 80% of "my water pressure dropped" calls are aerators or cartridge filters in the supply, not actual pressure issues. Five-minute fix. People spend thousands on pressure-boost pumps that didn't need to exist.
  • Garbage disposals run with the wrong food. Coffee grounds, fibrous vegetable peels, eggshells, and pasta are the four worst. We've cleared dozens of jammed disposals where the fix is a hex-wrench reset and a clean-out — no replacement needed.

Tools and skills required

Real residential plumbing fixes need: a basin wrench (for the impossibly-placed nuts behind the sink), channel-lock pliers, a closet auger (toilet snake) for clogged toilets, a hand auger for sinks, teflon tape, plumber's putty, silicone, a torque-controlled approach to compression fittings, and the experience to know when something is past handyman scope. The diagnostic skill is the bigger piece: knowing whether a wet spot under a sink is a supply leak, a drain leak, a P-trap leak, or condensation from cold-water lines in a humid climate. They look identical to a homeowner; they're four different fixes.

Safety, code, and licensing considerations

This is where we draw a clear line. Every state we operate in regulates plumbing differently — Florida, Texas, Georgia, Pennsylvania, North Carolina, DC, and Illinois all require a state plumbing license for work past the fixture connection. We operate as a handyman service: we work on the visible, fixture-level scope (everything from the angle stop forward to the faucet/showerhead/toilet/disposal). Anything that requires opening a wall to re-pipe, soldering inside structural framing, sewer-main work, gas-line work, or water-heater gas connection — we either partner with or refer to a licensed plumber. We tell you up front, every time, so you're not surprised. If you ever hear a contractor offer to do gas work or main-line repipe without a permit, that's the moment to walk away.

FAQ

Plumbing Questions

What plumbing work do you handle vs. when do I need a licensed plumber?

We handle the full range of fixture-level work — faucets, toilets, supply lines, shutoffs, garbage disposals, P-traps, drain snaking, and similar. We don't open walls to repipe, we don't touch the main sewer line past the first cleanout, and we don't work on gas lines (gas is a licensed-plumber/licensed-installer scope by code in every state we serve). When a job crosses into licensed-plumber territory, we tell you up front and refer to a partner.

My toilet is running constantly — how much to fix?

Almost always a $20 part: either the flapper, the fill valve, or both. Replacement takes 15–25 minutes. As a member, this is the kind of fix that's typically absorbed into your monthly visit. Drop-in price is $80–$120 plus parts.

Can you snake a clogged drain?

Yes — bathroom sink, kitchen sink, tub, and shower. We carry a 25-foot hand auger and a 50-foot drum auger. If a clog is past 50 feet (main line) or there's evidence of a sewer-line break, we recommend a sewer-rooter specialist with a camera — that's outside the handyman scope.

Will you install my new faucet or showerhead?

Yes. Single-hole, three-hole, widespread, wall-mount — all of it. We also redo the water-supply braided lines if yours are older than 10 years (they fail catastrophically when they fail). Showerheads, hand-held sprayers, and bidet attachments — same. We bring teflon tape, plumber's putty, and a basin wrench.

What if I have a leak? Is that an emergency?

Active dripping or pooling water is an emergency. First step: shut off the supply at the angle stop or the main if needed, put towels down, and call. Gold members get 4-hour emergency dispatch, Silver 12-hour. We carry SharkBite couplings, PEX, and copper repair kits — most leaks under sinks or in accessible walls are fixed in one visit.

Do you replace water heaters?

For electric water heaters, yes — straightforward swap of a 40 or 50 gallon tank. For gas water heaters and tankless installations, we partner with a licensed plumber because gas connection and combustion-air requirements need a permit and a state-licensed installer. We coordinate the whole job either way.
