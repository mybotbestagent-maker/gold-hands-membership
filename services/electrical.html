Safety First

Electrical is the one category where confidence kills people.

More than half the home electrical fires in the US start with a connection problem in a switch box, an outlet, or a fixture — wires that came loose, were backstabbed, or were wire-nutted dirty. We do this work every day, with proper torque settings on the screws, and we know which jobs are above our pay grade.

If you smell burning plastic or notice a warm switch/outlet: Turn off that breaker immediately, do not use the circuit, and call us for same-day diagnosis. This is a leading cause of house fires and is not a wait-until-tomorrow problem.

The DIY pitfalls we see every week

  • Backstabbed connections that fail under load. The "push-in" backstab holes on cheap outlets are a known failure point — they make a smaller contact patch than a screw terminal and they loosen over time, especially on circuits with hair dryers or heaters. We always wire to the screw terminals (or use a CO/ALR-rated outlet on aluminum wiring, which most pre-1972 homes have).
  • Ceiling fans hung from light-fixture boxes that aren't fan-rated. A standard light-fixture box is rated for 35 lb static — a fan with motor torque doubles that effective load. The result is a fan that wobbles, then drops out of the ceiling. The box has to be a fan-rated box stamped accordingly. We always check before mounting.
  • Dimmers paired with non-dimmable LED bulbs. The buzzing, flickering, and partial-on behavior that drives people insane is almost always a compatibility mismatch. Modern LED dimmers (Lutron Maestro, Caséta) work with most LEDs but you still have to confirm the bulb is dimmable. Standard wall dimmers from 1995 won't work with modern LED loads at all.
  • Aluminum wiring assumed to be copper. Homes built between 1965 and 1973 often have aluminum branch wiring, which oxidizes and creates resistance at every connection. Standard outlets and switches are not rated for aluminum — you need CO/ALR rated devices or AlumiConn lugs. Mismatched outlets on aluminum wire is a known fire hazard.
  • The "neutral isn't really hot" assumption. Wiring is hot whenever a circuit upstream is closed. Neutrals can carry current. Ground wires can become hot under fault conditions. We always test every wire with a non-contact tester and a multimeter before touching anything, even if "we're sure" the breaker is off.

Tools and skills required

Doing electrical work safely needs: a non-contact AC voltage tester (Klein NCVT-3 or Fluke), a multimeter, lineman's pliers, wire strippers calibrated for solid and stranded copper, a torque screwdriver (yes — outlet terminal screws have a torque spec, around 12 in-lb), wire nuts in red/orange/yellow with the right gauge mapping, and electrical tape rated 600V. The skill part: knowing how to identify the line, load, traveler, and neutral on a 3-way switch (the wires are the same color from the wall — you have to test); knowing when to stop and call a licensed electrician (anything that needs a permit or a panel-side change); and knowing every state's rules on what handyman-scope vs. licensed-scope work looks like.

FAQ

Electrical Questions

What electrical work do you handle vs. when do I need a licensed electrician?

We handle the visible scope: light fixtures, ceiling fans, switches, outlets, USB receptacles, dimmers, smart-home switch swaps, doorbell transformers, and similar like-for-like replacements. We do not pull permits, run new circuits, work inside the breaker panel, or modify service entrance — those require a licensed electrician under the National Electrical Code (NEC) and your state's licensing rules. We tell you up front when a job needs licensed scope.

Can you install a smart switch like Lutron Caséta or Kasa?

Yes. Smart switches need a neutral wire at the box (most homes built after 1985 have this; many older homes don't). We test for neutral first, and if it's not present we either recommend a no-neutral switch like the Lutron Caséta Diva or refer to a licensed electrician for a wire pull. We also program the switch to your hub before we leave.

Will you install a ceiling fan where there's currently no ceiling box?

Like-for-like swap of an existing fan, yes. Adding a new ceiling fan where there's no box at all (or where the existing box isn't fan-rated) requires running new wire and that crosses into licensed-electrician scope. Most existing light-fixture boxes are NOT rated for fan loads — we always check the rating stamped on the box before mounting.

Why is my outlet warm to the touch?

That's a serious sign. A warm outlet means high resistance somewhere — usually a loose wire, a backstabbed connection that's failing, or an overloaded circuit. Stop using it immediately and call us. We can check and replace the outlet (often the fix), but if the heat traces back into the wall we hand off to a licensed electrician. Hot outlets cause house fires every year — this is not a wait-and-see issue.

Do you replace recessed lights with LED upgrades?

Yes — LED retrofit kits drop into existing recessed cans in 5 minutes per fixture. Single-switch dim controls work with most major brands (Lutron, Leviton, Lithonia). For homes still running old halogen recessed lights, the energy savings alone usually pay back the labor in 18–24 months.

Can you fix flickering lights?

It depends on cause. Most flickering is one of: bad bulb, dimmer/LED incompatibility, loose wire at the fixture, or worn switch. We diagnose and fix the first three. Flicker that's coming from the panel side (multiple rooms at once, or correlated with large appliance startup) means a service-entrance or panel issue and we refer to a licensed electrician — that's a safety call, not a punt.
